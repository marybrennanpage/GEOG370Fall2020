In this project I learned how to display images in different projections

By: Mary Page

How I displayed the map in different projections using QGIS:

Using QGIS, I was able to display the map using different projections. First, I downloaded the data from Sakai to my desktop. After I saved and unzipped the files, I opened QGIS to begin working. Once in QGIS, I uploaded the ne_10m_admin_0_countries.shp (shapefile). Then I was able to click on the EPSG on the bottom right of the screen to adjust/change the EPSG for the desired coordinate reference system. For example, when I wanted to create the Pseudo-Mercator Projection, I clicked on the EPSG and filtered for "3857", and selected the Pseudo-Mercator coordinate reference system. After selecting the desired coordinate reference system, I used the Indicatrix Mapper plug-in, running the defaults, which added indicatrix circles over the projections. The indicatrix circles are useful to see the distortions that each projection cause.

My Projections:

WGS84 Projection

The indicatrix circles lose their shape the farther North and South they go. That is, while at the equator the Indicatrix circles are circles, the farther North and South you go, they turn into ellipses. This means that the projection does not preserve shape.

Aitoff Projection

It appears that shape and area are distorted. However, it appears that it is the least distorted at the Prime Meridian. The farther North or South you go, the larger the circles get, suggesting distortion in area, and the farther East or West you go, the circles start to change into ellipses, suggesting distortion in shape.

Pseudo Mercator Projection

The indicatrix circles get larger towards the North and South poles, suggesting that the projections conserves shape but that area is distorted.

Winkel I Projection

Similarly to the Aitoff Projection, it appears that the farther North or South you go, the larger the circles get, suggesting distortion in area, and the farther East or West you go, the circles start to change into ellipses, suggesting distortion in shape. However, the distortion doesn't seem as drastic as in the Aitoff Projection, as the circles aren't as drastically distorted.

Equal Area Cylindrical Projection

The indicatrix circles suggest that there isn't any area distortion but that there is shape distortion. It appears that there is shape distortion, as the farther North or South you go, the circles start to change into ellipses.

Equidistant Conic Projection

Shape and area disortion appears to increase farther from the center of the projection.

Azimuthal Equidistant Projection

Similarly to the Equidistant Conic Projection, shape and area disortion appears to increase farther from the center of the projection.

World Robinson Projection

The World Robinson Projection looks similiar to the Winkel I Projection. It appears that distortion increases closest to the poles. This projection distorts both shape and area, as the circles get larger and turn into slight ellipses closer to the poles.

World Hotine Projection

This Projection appears to have both shape and area distortion. It appears that the projection conserves area and shape fairly well until it nears the poles, where the area and shape distortion are both significant, as the ellipses are much larger than the other circles/slight ellipses seen in the projection.
